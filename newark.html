Newark Liberty Airport Suffers Third System Outage in Less Than Two Weeks

Backup air traffic control system failure causes delays and cancellations as Transportation Secretary announces flight reductions

CBS News NY Times CNN

Key Facts

What You Need to Know:

  • Newark Liberty International Airport experienced its third system outage in less than two weeks
  • The latest outage involved a momentary failure of a backup air traffic control system
  • The FAA implemented a 45-minute ground stop to ensure systems were functioning properly
  • Over 250 flights were delayed and at least 80 were canceled due to the outage
  • Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy has announced plans to temporarily reduce flight capacity at Newark

Newark Liberty International Airport (EWR) has been hit by yet another equipment outage, the third in less than two weeks, causing widespread delays and flight cancellations over the weekend. The Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) confirmed that Sunday's outage involved a momentary failure of a backup air traffic control system, prompting a 45-minute ground stop to ensure the system was functioning properly.

Unlike the previous two outages, which originated at the Philadelphia facility that now controls Newark's operations, this latest incident affected a backup system. While the FAA stated that operations returned to normal after the ground stop, the impact cascaded throughout the day, resulting in more than 250 flight delays and at least 80 cancellations affecting both domestic and international travelers.

Recent System Outages

The latest incident follows a troubling pattern of technical failures at Newark. On Friday, May 9, controllers at the Philadelphia facility lost radio contact and radar for about 90 seconds in the early morning hours. Prior to that, on April 28, a more severe outage occurred during a busy afternoon period, when controllers temporarily lost both radar and radio communication with aircraft.

The April 28 outage was particularly concerning, with multiple sources reporting that at least five air traffic controllers took 45 days of trauma leave following the incident. Pilots and controllers have expressed growing frustration with the situation, with one pilot radioing to a controller on Sunday: "Newark, just so you know, we're all rooting for you for better equipment and more staffing."

These technical failures come after the FAA moved Newark's air traffic control operations from a facility in Long Island, New York, to one in Philadelphia last summer. The move was intended to help with controller recruitment by relocating to a more affordable area. However, as of March 2024, only 59 percent of available controller positions were filled at the facility that previously handled flights for Newark and other New York area airports.

Government Response

In response to the ongoing issues, Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy announced plans to temporarily reduce the number of flights at Newark Airport. "We're going to have this reduced capacity at Newark. I'm convening a meeting of all the airlines that serve Newark, get them to agree on how they're gonna reduce the capacity," Duffy said at a news conference on Monday.

Duffy also outlined infrastructure improvements to address the technical problems, saying, "We are building a new line that goes directly from Newark to the Philly TRACON, which controls the New York airspace. What happens now is it goes from Newark to N90, which is where it used to be controlled, and then down to Philly. That doesn't make sense. We're gonna have a direct line there."

The Department of Transportation will conduct a "delay reduction meeting" on Wednesday with all airlines operating out of Newark to work on eliminating additional problems. The FAA has also created an "emergency task force" comprised of experts from the FAA, L3 Harris Corporation, and Verizon to ensure Newark operates safely and efficiently.

Additionally, Duffy announced that the FAA implemented a software update on Friday night to prevent future outages, and that fiberoptic cables have been installed and are being tested at Newark, JFK, and LaGuardia airports.

Political Dimension

The Newark situation has become politically charged, with Secretary Duffy placing blame on the previous Biden administration. "I think it is clear that the blame belongs with the last administration," he said during Monday's press conference. "Pete Buttigieg and Joe Biden did nothing to fix the system that they knew was broken."

Duffy has requested that the inspector general open an investigation "into the failures of the last administration" and the move of controllers from New York to Philadelphia. "There was a number of things that weren't done in preparation for the move, which has left us in the situation in which we now have," he stated. "What I'm criticizing is the fact that I don't believe that telecom was adequately tested."

In response, Chris Meagher, an adviser to former Transportation Secretary Pete Buttigieg, said that Duffy "needs to spend more time doing what the American people are paying him to do — fix problems — and less time blaming others."

Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer has called for quick action, stating, "The FAA must put Newark first and must put everything they've got into fixing Newark ASAP. This situation cannot last. We need all hands on deck at the FAA."

Impact on Travelers

Passengers have expressed growing frustration with the situation at Newark. Teah Thompson, who was traveling to Georgia with her four children, told CBS News, "We didn't think we were going to be here delayed and not at home already. We have to stay overnight until 12 p.m. tomorrow."

Keirsten Jones of Los Angeles noted it was the "second delay we are experiencing within three delays," while another traveler simply stated, "This airport is horrible."

Some frequent flyers are reconsidering their use of Newark altogether. Melissa Rodriguez, 46, of Bayonne, N.J., who regularly flies from Newark because of its proximity, told the New York Times she is now considering using other New York area airports after chaperoning a cheerleading team that landed at Newark on Monday.

"Everybody got either delayed or their flights were canceled, and this was going both ways," Rodriguez said. "They were very unhappy." She mentioned looking into flights arriving at Kennedy or LaGuardia as a backup, adding, "With all the problems at Newark, you've got to look at plans A, B, C, and D."

Safety Assurances

Despite the ongoing issues, officials have emphasized that flying in and out of Newark remains safe. United Airlines CEO Scott Kirby sent an email to customers on Monday reassuring them that all flights to and from Newark are "absolutely safe."

"When there are F.A.A. issues, such as technology outages or staffing shortages, the F.A.A. requires all airlines to fly fewer aircraft to maintain the highest levels of safety," Kirby wrote.

Acting FAA Administrator Chris Rocheleau also stressed at Monday's news conference that flying at the airport is safe. Secretary Duffy echoed this sentiment, stating, "Our commitment is always safety. We're going to make sure that if you fly, you're going to fly safely. And if we reduce the number of flights at Newark, we're not doing it to annoy people. We're not doing it to delay people's travel. What we're doing is guaranteeing safety."
