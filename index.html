Senate Passes New Infrastructure Bill with Bipartisan Support

The $1.2 trillion legislation represents a major investment in America's roads, bridges, public transit, and broadband internet

Reuters AP WSJ CNN

Key Facts

What You Need to Know:

  • The Senate passed a $1.2 trillion infrastructure bill with a 69-30 vote, showing rare bipartisan cooperation
  • The bill includes $550 billion in new spending on transportation, utilities, and pollution cleanup
  • Key investments include $110 billion for roads and bridges, $66 billion for passenger and freight rail, and $65 billion for broadband internet
  • The bill now moves to the House of Representatives for consideration
  • President is expected to sign the legislation if it passes the House

In a significant showing of bipartisan cooperation, the Senate has passed a $1.2 trillion infrastructure bill, marking a major step toward fulfilling a key legislative priority for the administration. The vote, which ended with 69 senators in favor and 30 opposed, demonstrates that bipartisan agreement is still possible on major legislation despite the highly polarized political climate.

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act represents the largest investment in America's physical infrastructure in decades. The bill includes approximately $550 billion in new federal spending over five years, with the remainder coming from the reallocation of existing funds.

The legislation now moves to the House of Representatives, where its passage may face additional challenges. Some progressive Democrats have indicated they won't support the infrastructure bill unless it moves in tandem with a larger social policy and climate bill that Democrats plan to pass through the budget reconciliation process, which doesn't require Republican support.

What's in the Bill

The infrastructure package includes significant investments in various sectors of American infrastructure:

Transportation: The bill allocates $110 billion for roads and bridges, $66 billion for passenger and freight rail, $39 billion for public transit, $25 billion for airports, and $17 billion for ports.

Utilities: The legislation includes $65 billion for broadband internet, $65 billion for the power grid, and $55 billion for water infrastructure.

Environmental Remediation: The bill provides $21 billion for environmental cleanup, addressing pollution and contaminants in soil and groundwater.

Electric Vehicles: The legislation allocates $7.5 billion for a network of electric vehicle charging stations and another $7.5 billion for electric buses and ferries.

How It's Funded

Unlike many large spending bills, the infrastructure package includes provisions designed to offset its costs, though the Congressional Budget Office has estimated it will add about $256 billion to the deficit over the next decade.

Funding sources in the bill include:

- Repurposing unspent COVID-19 relief funds
- Delaying a Medicare rebate rule
- Applying new reporting requirements for cryptocurrency transactions
- Extending certain government fees
- Strategic petroleum reserve sales
- Spectrum auctions for 5G services

The bill does not include corporate or individual tax increases, which had been a red line for Republican negotiators.

Political Analysis

The bipartisan nature of the Senate vote represents a significant achievement in today's polarized political climate. Nineteen Republican senators, including Minority Leader Mitch McConnell, joined all 50 Democrats in supporting the bill.

For the President, the bill's passage is a step toward fulfilling a campaign promise to work across the aisle and demonstrate that government can still function effectively. The administration has argued that the infrastructure investments will create jobs, enhance American competitiveness, and address climate change.

Republican supporters have emphasized the benefits of infrastructure improvements for their constituents and the economy, while arguing that they successfully prevented more ambitious Democratic spending proposals from being included in the bill.

What's Next

The bill now moves to the House of Representatives, where its fate is tied to a separate $3.5 trillion budget resolution focused on social programs, healthcare, and climate initiatives. Progressive Democrats have indicated they won't support the infrastructure bill unless the larger package moves forward simultaneously.

House Speaker Nancy Pelosi has suggested that the chamber won't take up the infrastructure bill until the Senate passes the larger package through budget reconciliation, a process that allows legislation to pass with a simple majority, avoiding the 60-vote threshold typically required to overcome a filibuster.

If the bill ultimately passes both chambers, it would represent one of the most significant investments in American infrastructure in generations and a rare example of bipartisan cooperation on major legislation.
